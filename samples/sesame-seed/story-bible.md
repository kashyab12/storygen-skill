# Story Bible: Pip of Bramblewick Farm

## Concept
- Chosen concept: The smallest seed on the farm dreams of being planted in
  the Big Field like the big seeds, gets swept out into the world, and
  discovers that smallness is a way of being mighty — and that belonging is
  the friends who grow toward you.
- Rejected candidates (and why):
  - *Kishōtenketsu seasons piece* (a year in the seed shed, twist: the jar
    is a pantry, not a prison) — too quiet; no journey for a read-aloud
    adventure about "adventures with friends."
  - *Braided seed + farm-girl story* — splits attention; the brief centers
    the seed and its friends.
  - *Sesame seed on a burger-bun odyssey* — funny but ends in being eaten;
    wrong emotional truth for a growing-up story.

## Logline
Pip, the smallest seed on Bramblewick Farm, wants to be planted in the Big
Field like the big seeds, but when a draft sweeps Pip into the farmyard on
the eve of the last frost, Pip must cross a world of giants with two lost
friends before the planting rain ends — or watch everyone's spring wash away.

## Arc
- Shape: Classic Arc (Freytag)
- Why: read-aloud children's adventure for ages 5–8; clean rising action,
  one climax, warm resolution is the right shape.

## Story spine
- **Once upon a time** there was a sesame seed named Pip, the smallest seed
  in the seed shed of Bramblewick Farm, where anything will grow.
- **Every day** the seeds talked about the Big Field they'd be planted in —
  but sesame seeds never went to the Big Field; they went into the kitchen
  jar on the windowsill.
- **One day**, on sorting morning, a draft swept Pip off the sorting table,
  through the crack under the door, and into the farmyard — with the last
  frost coming that very night, and the planting rain due to end at sunset
  the next day.
- **Because of that**, Pip had to cross the whole farmyard to reach the Big
  Field before the rain ended — and met Bean and Kernel, lost like Pip, and
  after a hard word and a frost night huddled under a dock leaf, they went
  on together.
- **Because of that**, at the hen run Pip had to do the one thing Pip never
  does — step out of the shadows where small things hide — and each
  friend's weakness needed another friend's strength.
- **Until finally**, at the flooding furrows, with Bean and Kernel watching
  from the mud, Pip had to choose: catch the gust into the Big Field at
  last, or let the dream go and plug the leak in the little earth dam — a
  gap only a sesame seed could fill — before their furrow drained onto the
  gravel where nothing grows.
- **And ever since then** — after one long night in the hedgerow corner
  when Pip thought everything was lost — Pip grows at the edge of it all,
  the tallest surprise on Bramblewick Farm, a hundred pods rattling like
  maracas; Bean's vines and Kernel's stalks lean toward Pip's corner, and
  every spring, Pip's hundred children are planted in the Big Field.

## Audience & format
- Audience: children 5–8, read-aloud (bedtime or storytime)
- Purpose: entertain; emotional truth — being small doesn't mean mattering
  less; you grow up the moment you choose someone else's spring over your
  own dream
- Format & length: prose storybook, ~1,400 words, rhythm and repetition as
  features (refrain, planned recurrences below)
- Tone boundaries: never scary past a gasp; peril resolves within a page;
  no death, no eating of named characters; never preachy

## Refrain plan
"Small as a freckle, light as a wish." Four placements, one transformation:
1. S1 — how the shed describes Pip (it stings; said *about* Pip)
2. S3 — the leaf-boat (it helps; said *by Kernel*, kindly, first time it's
   a compliment)
3. S7 — the climax turn (being light as a wish is what lets Pip ride the
   runnel to the dam — and letting the wish go is the choice)
4. S8 — transformed: "small as a freckle, light as a wish — and taller than
   the fence post," as the farm describes the plant Pip becomes

## World seed
- Setting: a small mixed farm ("Bramblewick Farm, where anything will
  grow") with a defining twist: to a seed, the farmyard is a whole world —
  a puddle is a sea, a hen is a giant, a bootprint is a valley
- Inhabitants: seeds (talking, only to each other), hens, crows, one cat,
  unseen humans (Farmer Meg — boots and hands only)
- Runs on: the planting calendar — soil, sun, and the spring rain
- Struggle: seeds rely on the planting rain but can't move far on their
  own; a seed that misses its chance waits a whole year in the open

## World rules
1. Seeds can talk to each other, roll, hop, and cling — but only travel far
   by wind, water, feather, or boot. (Established S1–2; never bent.)
2. A seed that is settled in soil by the time the planting rain ends will
   grow; a seed that isn't must wait a whole year — outside, in the open,
   with the crows. (The clock and the stake. Established S2.)
3. Soil is only where soil is: the furrows, the hedgerow bank, the garden
   beds. Flagstones, gravel, and the yard are barren. (Established S2 —
   this is why washing out of a furrow onto the gravel path is the danger.)
4. On a frost night, a seed alone in the open gets too cold to ever grow
   tall; seeds keep warm the old way — packed close together. (Established
   S2 by Kernel, used S4. Gentle peril: cold makes you small forever, it
   doesn't kill you.)
5. Humans and animals can't hear seeds. (Established S1.)
6. Bramblewick Farm grows anything — its name and its nature. (Established
   S1; pays off S8: a sesame plant thrives in the hedgerow corner.)
7. Grown plants can't walk — they lean. (Stated S8; it's why Pip's choice
   is forever, and why the friends leaning toward the corner means what it
   means.)

## Characters
### Pip — protagonist, sesame seed
- Wants: to be planted in the Big Field like the big seeds
- Needs: to learn worth isn't weighed in ounces, and belonging isn't a
  place — it's who grows toward you
- Strengths: quick, light, fits anywhere, notices everything low-down
- Weaknesses: measures worth in size; **hides in shadows when ashamed or
  scared** (operative: hides after Bean's tease in S3; must step into the
  open at the hen run in S5 — Pip's first real choice, before the dam)
- Core quality: too small to be noticed, too determined to be stopped
- Motivations & backstory: a whole winter on the shed shelf hearing pumpkin
  seeds brag about acreage; the jar on the windowsill glinting like a
  sentence
- Voice: short eager sentences; **measures self against things** ("taller
  than a raindrop, almost"; "half a ladybird, standing up") — hopeful
  measuring, never Kernel's numbers
- Arc: "I'm too small to matter" → "small is how I saved the spring"
- Knows (updated per scene): S1 jar-fate; S2 the clock, the gravel rule,
  "cracks are for small ones"; S3 friends' names; S6 how furrow dams work;
  S7 the gap's size

### Bean — runner bean seed, friend
- Wants: to be the first vine up the tallest pole
- Strengths: big (seed-big), bold, loud
- Weaknesses: afraid of heights — a terrible secret for a climbing bean;
  bravado when scared; **speaks before he thinks** (the tease in S3:
  "You're barely a crumb!" — he means it as a joke; it lands like a stone)
- Core quality: loudest heart on the farm
- Voice: exclamations, dares, "Easy-peasy!" (says it more the less easy it
  is)
- Arc: bravado and a careless tease → a real apology (frost night, S4) →
  one honest "I'm scared" → climbs anyway (S5)
- Knows: S3 that his joke hurt; S4 how to say sorry; S5 that Pip saw him
  freeze and didn't laugh

### Kernel — sweetcorn seed, friend
- Wants: to get to the furrow without being eaten
- Strengths: knows every farm fact and danger by heart — his worry is a map
- Weaknesses: worry stacks up until he can't roll; round (rolls too well
  downhill)
- Core quality: the worrier who shows up anyway
- Voice: facts and numbers delivered *fearfully* ("Crows eat forty seeds a
  day. Forty!") — anxious counting, never Pip's hopeful measuring
- Arc: worry as a wall → worry as a map — his facts save the group three
  times (frost-huddle S4, feeding-hour S5, dam mechanics S6)
- Knows: S4 frost lore; S5 the hens' feeding hour; S6 how Farmer Meg banks
  the little earth dams

### Grandmother Sunflower — mentor (appears S1 only, once)
- Role: oldest seed in the shed, saved for seed year after year
- Core quality: patience of someone who has been a flower already
- Mentor rule: guides once, never rescues. Her line (S1): "A seed's worth
  isn't weighed in ounces, little one. It's counted in springs." In S8 the
  line returns **refracted through Pip's own voice** (counting the friends'
  springs Pip made possible), never repeated verbatim.

## Open threads
- Grandmother's line (S1) → refracted S8, never verbatim
- The kitchen jar glinting (S1) → reframed S8: Bramblewick keeps seed from
  its best plants — Pip's children fill next year's packets, not the jar
- "Cracks are for small ones" (S2, the door crack) → pays off S7 (the
  fence-post crack catch)
- Bean's tease (S3) → apology (S4) → trust (S5)
- Bean's secret fear (S3 hinted) → paid S5 (the climb)
- Kernel's facts mocked in the shed (S1, pumpkin seeds laugh) → his facts
  save the group three times (S4, S5, S6)
- Refrain: four placements per the refrain plan
- The rattling pods "like maracas" (S8) — echo of Pip measuring hop-lengths
  (S2)

## Continuity watchlist
- The clock: frost is Day 1 night; rain starts Day 2 midday (early —
  forecast said later), ends at sunset Day 2. Never contradict.
- Pip never *settles into* growing soil until the hedgerow bank (S7, end).
  The earth dam (S7) is not settling: Pip is wedged in the wall, held by
  water pressure, and lifted out before the rain ends — rule 2 untouched.
- Stakes plumbing: if the dam drains, Bean & Kernel wash onto the *gravel
  path* (rule 3: barren; rule 2: a year in the open with crows). Pip's
  sacrifice = the Big Field, forever (rule 7: plants lean, don't walk).
- Bean is seed-big but yard-small: too big for the gate gap, small enough
  to hide under a dock leaf.
- Hens are penned; they menace only near the run, and only to a gasp.
- Farmer Meg is only ever boots, hands, and a voice from above.
- Pip's measuring tic and Kernel's counting tic must never blur: Pip
  measures hopefully (against things), Kernel counts fearfully (dangers).
