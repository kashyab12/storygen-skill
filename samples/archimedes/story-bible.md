# Story Bible: The Circles of Syracuse

## Concept
- Chosen concept: The life of Archimedes told as a circular narrative on
  the last morning of his life, during the Roman sack of Syracuse (212 BC).
  An old man draws circles in the sand of his courtyard; each circle opens
  a ring of memory — the sand of his boyhood, Alexandria, the crown, the
  lever, the siege — while between the rings the frame's clock ticks: the
  sack comes up the street. Frame and memory converge on the soldier in
  the doorway and the famous last line. Coda: Cicero finds the tomb 137
  years later.
- Rejected candidates (and why):
  - *Proposition-shaped biography* (each chapter a lemma with QED) — formally
    delicious but keeps the reader at arm's length from the man.
  - *Straight chronology, birth to death* — the death is the strongest
    material; saving it for the end of a chronology makes the middle sag.
  - *POV of the Roman soldier throughout* — strong frame, but the soldier
    can't see Alexandria or the bath; too much would be secondhand.
  - *Narrated by water* (bath → harbor → siege) — clever, risks preciousness
    across 2,000 words.

## Logline
On the morning Syracuse falls, the greatest mind of the ancient world wants
only to finish one last proof, but the war he spent two years holding back
with iron and geometry has finally come through his door — and he must
choose, as he has chosen all his life, between the world and the circle,
knowing at last that they were never different things.

## Arc
- Shape: Circular Narrative
- Why: the story begins and ends with a circle drawn in sand; the ending
  returns to the opening image with its meaning transformed (a doodle
  becomes a last testament; the tomb's sphere-in-cylinder closes the loop).
  The life is delivered as rings within the frame, not a march of years.

## Story spine
- **Once upon a time** there was an old man in a courtyard in Syracuse,
  drawing a circle in a tray of sand while the city fell around him.
- **Every day** of his seventy-five years, the world had interrupted the
  circle — kings, crowns, wars — and every day he had gone back to it,
  because only the circle was permanent; and the people who loved him paid
  for what the circle got.
- **One day** — this day — Rome finally breached the walls his machines had
  held for two years, and the interruption coming up his street carried a
  sword.
- **Because of that**, each circle he drew that morning opened a ring of
  memory — the boy counting sand, Alexandria and the friends who became
  unanswered letters, the naked run from the bath, the lever and the
  launched ship, the claw — while between rings the sack came closer:
  smoke, then screams, then hobnails on the paving stones.
- **Because of that**, tracing the rings, he arrived at the one thing he
  had never proven: the night the Romans fled from a rope and his gunners
  laughed on the wall, he had heard what the machines really were — the
  only letters he ever managed to write to the living. The pure and the
  practical had never been enemies.
- **Until finally**, the shadow fell across the sand — and knowing now what
  circles were for, he did the two things at once that he had never managed
  in seventy-five years: he sent Dexios out the back with the tablets
  (choosing a person), and he asked the sword to wait for a proof (choosing
  the circle). "Do not disturb my circles." The price was the price.
- **And ever since then**, the circle has held: a hundred and thirty-seven
  years later a Roman — Cicero — cut back the brambles from a forgotten
  grave and found a sphere inside a cylinder, the ratio still true; the
  sword is rust, and the proof is still standing where he told us to stand.

## Audience & format
- Audience: adult general reader; literary short fiction
- Purpose: move the reader — the cost and the gift of a mind that loved the
  eternal, without letting the reader off the hook about what it cost the
  people around him; transformation: the reader ends holding both awe and
  ache
- Format & length: prose short story, ~2,200 words; close third person on
  Archimedes for frame and rings; one deliberate beat in Marcus's POV at
  the doorway; coda in a cooler, historian's register
- Tone boundaries: no whimsy, no "great man" hagiography, no gore; the
  death happens in white space, not on the page

## World seed
- Setting: Syracuse under sack, 212 BC — with the defining twist that we
  experience the city's fall from inside one courtyard and one old man's
  refusal to look up
- Inhabitants: Archimedes; Dexios (his young amanuensis); the dead who live
  in the rings (Phidias, Conon, Hiero); Marcus, a Roman soldier
- Runs on: proof — the belief that some things, once shown, are true forever
- Struggle: the man who trusts only the eternal keeps being needed by the
  temporal — and the people who love him collect the debt

## World rules (historical contract)
1. Anchored in the sources (Plutarch, Vitruvius, Polybius, Livy, Cicero):
   the crown and the bath, "give me a place to stand," the Syracusia
   launch, the claw, the two-year siege, Marcellus' order to spare him,
   "do not disturb my circles" (traditional wording), the sphere-in-cylinder
   tomb, Cicero finding it in 75 BC. Never contradict a source.
2. Where sources conflict or are legend, the story chooses and signals it:
   - **Burning mirrors**: legend — appear only as a rumor Dexios repeats
     and Archimedes scoffs at.
   - **The death**: sources disagree (Plutarch's main version: the soldier
     didn't know him). CHOSEN VARIANT: Dexios, trying to shield him, cries
     the name — and the name is exactly what two years under the claw
     taught Marcus to fear and hate. Knowledge doesn't save him; it dooms
     him. The coda says plainly that the stories disagree about the
     doorway ("the words they gave him afterward") — honest signaling in
     the historian's register.
   - **The last words**: traditional, not certain — coda acknowledges.
3. Invention is licensed only in interiority and connective tissue (Dexios
   is invented; the specific last proof is invented — spirals, plausible
   given *On Spirals*, never claimed as fact in-text; boyhood scenes
   extrapolated from his father Phidias the astronomer).
4. Period discipline: no anachronisms of object or idea; Greek terms used
   sparingly and glossed by context. Eratosthenes reached Alexandria only
   c. 245 BC — he exists in this story ONLY as later correspondence, never
   bodily in the Ring 2 library.

## Characters
### Archimedes — protagonist, 75
- Wants: to finish the spiral proof in the sand tray before the war reaches
  him
- Needs: to see, before the end, that the machines — the "vulgar" work he
  apologized for all his life — were not a betrayal of geometry but its
  kindness; and to *act* on it once toward a living person (Dexios), not
  just conclude it privately
- Strengths: the purest geometric mind of antiquity; total absorption
- Weaknesses: absent from the living — forgets to eat, bathe, answer;
  lets letters from friends sit sealed while a proof is open; values the
  permanent so completely he shortchanges the momentary (people) — and the
  story keeps a ledger of who paid (mother, Conon, Dexios)
- Core quality: lives inside proofs the way other men live inside houses
- Motivations & backstory: son of Phidias the astronomer; the boy who asked
  how many grains of sand the universe could hold; Alexandria's library and
  the friends he afterward knew only as letters; recalled home to Hiero's
  Syracuse
- Voice: precise, dry, a little deaf to small talk; tenderness leaks out
  sideways in geometry ("a straight line is a circle grown patient") — he
  is the ONLY character who speaks in geometry
- Arc: "the circle instead of the world" → "the circle was how I held the
  world" — the reconciliation is *earned in S6 by a concrete memory* (the
  gunners' laughter) and *enacted in S7* (Dexios and the tablets), so the
  final refusal reads as a whole man's choice, not the old flaw winning
- Knows: from ring 5 on, that the city is lost; from the shadow on, that
  the man behind him will not wait

### Dexios — amanuensis, 16 (invented)
- Wants: to get the old man to the harbor and the boats before the Romans
  come
- Strengths: patient, loyal, quick; the only one who can read the master's
  sand-shorthand
- Weaknesses: too young to be listened to; loves the old man and can't say
  so; believes, fatally, that the great name is a shield
- Core quality: the living world's last ambassador to Archimedes
- Voice: logistics and time — tides, boats, hours, streets ("The harbor,
  master. The tide turns at noon.") He *translates* geometry into human
  terms; he never speaks in it. (Distinct channel from Archimedes.)
- Arc: begging him to run → being SEEN by him at last (the master uses his
  name, entrusts the tablets — the love finally lands on a living person) →
  carrying the proofs out through the back garden; his cry from the gate
  ("It's Archimedes!") is love, and it is the thing that kills the man it
  meant to save
- Knows: the sack's geography hour by hour; at the end, that he was seen

### Marcus — Roman legionary, 30s
- Wants: loot and a prisoner worth a reward; to go home
- Strengths: survivor of two years of the claw and the stones — he has
  earned his fear of this city's machines
- Weaknesses: illiterate in everything the old man is; a sword is his only
  sentence; two years of terror have fused the name "Archimedes" to the
  arm that came over the wall
- Core quality: not a monster — a tired man in the wrong doorway, which is
  worse
- Voice: soldier's inventory-speech — short declaratives, counts things
  (months of siege, men lost, the take from each house, years left on his
  oath); no metaphor except one, earned, at the sand tray
- Arc (one beat): inventory → an instant of almost-understanding at the
  sand tray (the one metaphor) → the name lands, and fear finishes the
  sentence his curiosity had started
- Knows: Marcellus wants the engineer alive; he does not connect "the
  engineer" to a barefoot old man in a courtyard — until Dexios's cry

### Hiero II — king of Syracuse (rings 4–5, dead by 215 BC)
- Wants: wonders from his kinsman — proof his little kingdom matters
- Core quality: shrewd affection; the only man who could interrupt the
  circle and be forgiven
- Voice: statecraft wrapped in family teasing; his ask for weapons is made
  as a half-joke over wine, never as a thesis ("Cousin, your circles are
  perfect and my walls are not. Introduce them.")

## Open threads
- The unfinished spiral in the sand tray (S1) → left unfinished (S7) → yet
  "finished" by the tomb's completed figure (S8)
- Sand: shore (S2) → sand tray (S1/S7) → the Sand-Reckoner glanced at in
  S6 — one grain-image threaded through
- "A place to stand" (S5) → final line of coda (S8)
- The flaw ledger: mother unanswered (S2) → Conon's sealed letter (S3) →
  Dexios unheard (frame) → PAID in S7: Dexios named, seen, entrusted
- The tablets (S1 gathered → frame ticks carried → S7 out the back garden
  → S8: how anything survived to be found — the iteration history saved)
- Hiero's half-joke (S5) → answered by the S6 epiphany and the S7 choice
- The mirror rumor (S6, scoffed) → coda names it among the legends kindly
- "Until noon" (frame tick 2) → S7 opens past noon: the tide has turned,
  the boats are gone — flight is no longer an option when he chooses to
  stay (the choice is real but its window was spent on the proof: both
  true at once)

## Continuity watchlist
- Dates: born c. 287 BC; Alexandria in youth (c. 265–260 — Eratosthenes
  NOT yet there; letters only, later); crown c. 250; Syracusia c. 240;
  Hiero dies 215 (absent from S7, dead before the sack); sack 212; Cicero
  75 BC = "a hundred and thirty-seven years"
- The claw and stone-throwers are documented; burning mirrors are legend —
  rumor only, scoffed at (S6), named as legend in coda
- Death-variant is CHOSEN and SIGNALED (rule 2): Dexios's cry names him;
  Marcus kills against Marcellus' order; coda flags that the stories
  disagree about the doorway
- The last proof is invented (spirals) — never claimed as fact in-text
- The frame ticks escalate strictly: smoke → screams → hobnails → gate →
  shadow; never out of order
- The tide clock: "noon" set at frame tick 2; S7 is past noon — boats gone
- Marcus never learns geometry; his one metaphor is his own (inventory-
  shaped), not borrowed from Archimedes
- No gore on the page; the sword falls in white space
- Voice channels never blur: geometry = Archimedes only; logistics/time =
  Dexios; inventory = Marcus; family-teasing statecraft = Hiero
