# Craft: What Makes Stories Great

Reference for the Plan and Write phases. These are the standards every draft
is built against and every Braintrust reviewer tests against.

## Character-driven narrative

The engine of a great story is a character, not a plot.

- **Vivid, deeply motivated, conflicted, and alive.** Every major character
  has a conscious desire (what they say they want), an unconscious need
  (what they actually need), and a flaw that puts the two in tension.
- **Driven by desire and conflict.** If you can't state what the protagonist
  wants in one sentence, you don't have a story yet.
- **Relatable over admirable.** The audience admires the *effort*, not the
  success. A character who struggles and fails forward beats a character who
  wins effortlessly, every time.
- **Characters must be tested.** Force them to grow through uncomfortable
  situations. If the protagonist could get what they want without changing,
  the story has no reason to exist.
- **Honesty in the unbelievable.** Premises can be impossible; emotions
  can't. Ground every fantastical element in a true human feeling.

## The story spine

The load-bearing skeleton (Pixar's formulation):

> **Once upon a time there was…** (world + protagonist in equilibrium)
> **Every day…** (the routine, what "normal" costs them)
> **One day…** (the inciting incident — irreversible)
> **Because of that…** (consequence, not sequence)
> **Because of that…** (escalating consequence)
> **Until finally…** (climax — the protagonist must choose)
> **And ever since then…** (the new equilibrium; what changed)

Rules of the spine:

1. Every "Because of that" must be **causal**. "And then" is a symptom of a
   broken spine. Test: could you reorder two beats without breaking anything?
   If yes, they aren't causally linked.
2. The "One day" must be irreversible — the protagonist cannot simply go home.
3. "Until finally" must be a *choice* the protagonist makes under maximum
   pressure, and the choice must be possible only because of how they've grown.
4. "And ever since then" must show a changed value: the world or the
   character is measurably different from "Once upon a time."

## Multi-layered construction

Great stories are built like great software: systems thinking AND craft, at
every layer simultaneously. Weakness at any layer breaks the whole.

| Layer | What it is | Failure smell |
|---|---|---|
| **Environment / setting** | Worldbuilding: rules, texture, logic of the place | Generic backdrop; rules bent for plot convenience |
| **Character development** | Desire, agency, arc | Passive protagonist; change that isn't earned |
| **Scene mechanics** | Beats, turning points | Scenes that don't change anything; flat middles |
| **Voice & dialogue** | Distinctive, purposeful | Interchangeable speakers; dialogue as exposition |

## Scene mechanics

A scene is a unit of change. For every scene, be able to answer:

- Whose scene is it, and what do they want *right now*?
- What's in the way?
- What **turns**? Name the value that flips (safety→danger,
  ignorance→knowledge, together→alone). No turn, no scene — cut it or merge it.
- What does the reader know at the end that they didn't at the start?

Enter scenes late, leave early. Start as close to the turn as possible.

## Voice & dialogue

- Each character should be identifiable from dialogue alone — cover the names
  and you should still know who's speaking (vocabulary, rhythm, what they
  avoid saying).
- Dialogue is action: characters speak to *get something*, not to inform the
  reader. Exposition goes in conflict or it goes out.
- Subtext over text. Characters rarely say exactly what they mean; the gap
  between what's said and what's meant is where the reader leans in.

## Format-specific notes

- **Children's / bedtime stories**: one clear emotional truth; repetition and
  rhythm are features; the child protagonist (or stand-in) must solve the
  problem themselves — adults may help, never rescue.
- **Teaching stories** (e.g. "teach my son to apologize"): never moralize
  directly. Let the character feel the consequence, discover the repair, and
  experience the relief of making it right. The lesson is the arc, not a
  closing sermon.
- **Tall tales**: exaggeration escalates beat by beat (big → huge →
  impossible), but the emotional core stays true and small.
- **Universe remixes** (e.g. Jake Paul vs. Toph): honor each source's canon
  rules; the fun is in the collision of logics, not the abandonment of them.
  Each universe's power system and tone constraints go in the story bible as
  world rules.
- **Scripts / screenplays**: everything must be visible or audible; no
  interiority that a camera can't capture. White space is pacing.
